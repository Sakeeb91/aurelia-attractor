\begin{abstract}
Random search has been generating chaotic systems since the early 1990s, and
evolutionary methods now breed them by the tens of thousands; what neither
provides is a reason to look at any particular specimen. We describe a
search whose objective is \emph{measured difference from everything already
known}: a quality-diversity (MAP-Elites) loop over a $C_n$-equivariant
family of three-dimensional polynomial flows, in which each candidate orbit
is reduced to a geometry-only shape fingerprint and scored by its distance
to the 135-system \textsf{dysts} catalog of published chaotic attractors.
Cells of the archive are indexed by symmetry order, Lyapunov exponent, and
body aspect, so the search illuminates the family rather than converging.
A first run evaluated 4{,}608 candidate systems in 127 seconds and retained
62 elites. We certify three exhibits --- Naiad ($C_2$), Aurelia ($C_3$),
and Cassiopea ($C_4$) --- with full Lyapunov spectra and error bars
($\lambda_1 = 0.294\pm0.009$, $0.230\pm0.009$, $0.523\pm0.008$), fractal
dimensions, equilibrium analyses, bifurcation scans, and a homoclinic hunt
that finds near-Shilnikov connections (return distances down to
$7.5\times10^{-5}$) threading two of the three. We validate the fingerprint
metric itself with positive and negative controls and an explicit noise
floor; all three systems sit 3--5 noise floors from anything in the
catalog. The exhibits are evidence for the method: catalog-grounded novelty
turns ``yet another chaotic system'' into a measurable, falsifiable claim
of isolation in shape space.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Strange attractors have been discovered mostly one at a time. Lorenz found
his by accident in a truncated convection model \citep{lorenz1963}; for
three decades afterwards each new chaotic flow was an event, named for its
finder. Sprott industrialized the process in 1994 by integrating millions
of random quadratic systems and keeping the nineteen algebraically simplest
chaotic ones \citep{sprott1994}, having already automated the analogous
hunt over two-dimensional maps with an explicitly aesthetic filter
\citep{sprott1993}. The genre this spawned eventually saturated: so many
cosmetic variants of known systems were published that Sprott himself
proposed editorial standards --- a new chaotic system should be simpler
than anything known with the same behavior, or show behavior of genuine
scientific interest \citep{sprott2011}. Modern evolutionary methods have
only widened the firehose: genetic programming evolves hundreds of
Lorenz-like variants by maximizing the largest Lyapunov exponent
\citep{panDas2015}, and the Panda corpus bred $2\times10^4$ chaotic
ordinary differential equations from catalog seeds as training data for a
forecasting foundation model \citep{lai2025panda}. Generating chaotic
systems is cheap. What remains expensive is a \emph{reason to look at any
particular one}.

This paper proposes that the missing objective is measured novelty. The
machine-readable \textsf{dysts} catalog \citep{gilpin2021} collects 135
published chaotic systems --- a century of the bestiary in one place ---
and makes a new kind of search target possible: not ``is this chaotic?''
(easy, and no longer interesting on its own) but ``how far is this, as a
geometric object, from everything anyone has published?'' We reduce every
candidate attractor to a cheap shape fingerprint (a D2 pairwise-distance
histogram \citep{osada2002} plus gross-anatomy features), define novelty
as the minimum fingerprint distance to the catalog, and make that number
the selection signal.

The second ingredient is a search algorithm that does not converge.
Optimizing novelty alone collapses easily onto degenerate weirdness ---
our own first run surfaced a nearly flat, barely chaotic sheet as its most
novel specimen --- and optimizing chaos strength alone reproduces the
firehose. Quality-diversity methods \citep{lehmanStanley2011,pugh2016},
specifically MAP-Elites \citep{mouretClune2015}, hold a map of cells and
keep the best specimen per cell; we index cells by symmetry order,
Lyapunov exponent, and body aspect, so the output is an \emph{atlas} of
distinct, certified, maximally unfamiliar attractors rather than one
optimum.

The third ingredient is structure. Rather than searching free-form
equations, we search a single $C_n$-equivariant family of
three-dimensional polynomial flows (Section~\ref{sec:family}), built from
the unique resonant coupling $\gamma\conj{w}^{\,n-1}$ of equivariant
bifurcation theory \citep{golubitskyStewartSchaeffer1988} joined to cubic
axial dynamics. The family is parameterized by the rotation order $n$, so
two-, three-, four-, and five-fold symmetric attractors live in one
seven-dimensional parameter box, and every find inherits an interpretable
anatomy: a single on-axis saddle-focus whose unstable spiral plane and
strongly stable axis organize a symmetric recirculation.

We present three certified exhibits --- Naiad ($C_2$), Aurelia ($C_3$),
Cassiopea ($C_4$) --- but we emphasize that, per Sprott's standard, the
exhibits are not the claim. The claim is the method: to our knowledge this
is the first quality-diversity search over a family of dynamical systems,
and the first use of catalog-grounded novelty as the selection objective
for discovering chaotic flows. Our contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a novelty objective grounded in a scientific catalog, with the
        metric itself validated by positive and negative controls and an
        explicit noise floor (Section~\ref{sec:map});
  \item an equivariant polynomial ansatz that turns symmetry order into a
        searchable discrete dimension (Section~\ref{sec:family});
  \item a MAP-Elites archive over symmetry $\times$ dynamics descriptors
        with a batched evaluator (4{,}608 candidates in 127\,s on a
        laptop; Section~\ref{sec:run});
  \item a certification standard for machine-discovered systems: full
        spectra with error bars over integrator settings and initial
        conditions, equilibrium analysis, bifurcation scans, and a
        homoclinic hunt (Sections~\ref{sec:convergence}--\ref{sec:shilnikov});
  \item three certified, named exhibits and two measured surprises ---
        chaos strength is not monotonic in symmetry order, and
        near-homoclinic Shilnikov windows thread two of the three
        families (Section~\ref{sec:nonmonotonic}).
\end{enumerate}
